Each line tells a story of terror, shifting tastes, and the ever-changing fears of humanity.Here we see the rise and fall of the great creatures that have haunted humanity's dreams. Once upon a time, in the early days of horror, ghosts ruled supreme. Mysterious, intangible, and steeped in folklore—they were the darlings of terror. But, as humanity grew bolder, more... material fears began to take hold. And finally, in the 50’s, our Master claims his rightful place! The vampire, elegant and eternal, rose to dominate the decade. Surely you’ve heard of a certain Dracula who contributed to this surge, no? Yet… as you can see, this dominance was fleeting. Soon, audiences grew distracted by new horrors—aliens, zombies, even clowns. Yes, clowns! Can you imagine anything so… undignified?

And then, of course, we have the witches—always lurking, always waiting for their moment in the shadows. And the aliens, with their cold, otherworldly menace. Both fascinating competitors, though I dare say they lack the allure of a finely tailored cape and a Transylvanian accent. Ah, but here lies the true challenge of our time: the zombie. From the 1990s onward, these shambling, brainless husks became the face of horror. Why? Perhaps because they reflect the fears of the modern world—pandemics, overpopulation, the loss of individuality. But where is the grace, the sophistication? Such crude creatures, don’t you think? Now, between you and me, Dracula does not take kindly to this chart. To think that his kind—the aristocrats of horror—must share the stage with these upstarts? It’s enough to make him… well, bite.

What’s your favorite killer like? Ah, that's the question, isn’t it? The language we use to describe our predators tells us so much about our deepest fears. As the decades pass, the word killer rises to dominance. There’s a steady progression in its prominence, especially in the 70s. By the 90s, it peaks—unsurprising, really. The world was gripped by the sensation of slasher films, and everyone, it seemed, had a killer in their sights. But then—ah, how the mighty fall. By the late 90s and 2000s, killer starts to fade in favor of other descriptors. A shift, perhaps, toward the more nuanced and sophisticated killer? Enter the serial killer, a term that climbs during the 80s and becomes iconic in the 90s and 2000s. A killer with method and madness! One can imagine Dracula, always fond of patterns, watching with glee as this term ascends. He is, of course, no stranger to serial behavior—though I’d say his taste is far more... refined.

And then we see the slow rise of the stalker, a term that peaks in the 2020s. It seems the world’s fears have evolved. It’s not just the killer who lurks in the shadows now—it’s the stalker, watching, waiting, obsessing. Perhaps a reflection of the growing sense of paranoia in our connected world, where privacy is no longer a given. But there’s one term that really stands out, isn't there? In the 80s, a new breed of killer emerges—predators. Finally, the butcher. A bit of an outlier, don’t you think? It peaks early in the 60s and 70s, then fades away into obscurity. Perhaps people grew tired of the brute force—looking for something a little more... elegant. Dracula would certainly approve. So, what do you think, my dear guest? Which killer appeals most to your taste? It seems the world’s idea of horror killers has evolved... perhaps in ways even Dracula did not anticipate.

Well, well, well. It seems horror films don’t just show us the terrors lurking in the shadows—they also reveal just how bizarre and twisted our desires can be. As you can see from this chart, the word love has consistently reigned supreme in horror film titles, almost always at the top of the list. A rather curious choice, wouldn’t you say? Perhaps it’s not the love we think of—no, no. In horror, love is often forbidden, twisted, or downright dangerous. Then we see desire making a strong presence, particularly in the 1950s. Could it be that post-war anxieties manifested in such yearnings? Sexy takes a turn around the 1970s, and by the 1990s, erotic surfaces—how charming. A shift towards more explicit themes, perhaps reflecting a society growing more... liberated in its darkest fantasies? As always, horror mirrors our strange and unsettling nature. It’s clear that, when it comes to the things that lurk in our minds, we’re not just afraid—we’re drawn to the bizarre, the taboo, the forbidden.
